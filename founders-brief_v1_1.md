## 1. North Star (the real goal)

Build a patient, high-trust, exceptionally organized beginner personal-finance resource that turns money fear and overwhelm into small, doable steps. Revenue is the lagging reward, not the lever. Secondary goal: learn a reusable AI-assisted publishing workflow that can transfer to future ventures.

Posture: slow asset, quality over volume, no urgent monetization. Realistic horizon to meaningful money is 12 to 24 months of consistent work.

---

## 2. Audience and pain points (the differentiation)

Target reader: a beginner who is anxious about or avoidant of personal finance, or who finds it overwhelming and does not know where to start. The big reference sites explain what things are; they do not meet this reader's fear. That gap is the edge.

The six pain points, which drive topic angles:
1. "I don't know where to start" paralysis.
2. Jargon shame (the vocabulary feels foreign, and not knowing it brings shame).
3. Avoidance and fear of looking (afraid to open the banking app).
4. Emotional roots, not math (money beliefs from upbringing and comparison culture).
5. Inertia and drift (procrastination whose cost is invisible future regret).
6. Relief comes from structure (small wins, tiny next steps, celebrate progress).

---

## 3. House voice

Calm like Morgan Housel, warm and shame-free like Erin Lowry, and more ruthlessly organized than either. We emulate technique and tone, never copy text. Style is not copyrightable; expression is. Originality is a hard gate (see rubric).

Voice rules:
1. Name the feeling in the first two sentences. Open on the reader's worry, not a definition.
2. Plain language only. Define any unavoidable jargon in the same breath.
3. One idea per article. If it needs two, it is two articles.
4. Anchor every abstract point with one small, concrete, original scenario.
5. Wise-friend tone. Warm, calm, never preachy, never shaming, never salesy.
6. Offer options, not commandments.
7. End with one tiny next step plus a short recap.

---

## 4. Content format and the great-result test

The "calm explainer" skeleton:
1. Hook that names the fear (2 to 3 sentences).
2. Reassurance ("you are not behind, here is the plan").
3. The one idea, in plain words, with a concrete scenario.
4. Ordered steps, short, each a single action.
5. One next step plus a 3-line recap.

Great result, in one test: a scared beginner finishes it feeling calmer and knowing their single next step, and a finance-savvy editor finds nothing inaccurate.

---

## 5. Quality rubric

**Hard gates (any failure blocks publishing):**
- G1 Accuracy. Every fact and number correct, current, and sourced. Nothing invented.
- G2 Educational, not advice, and safe. Includes a plain "general education, not personalized financial advice" line. Non-judgmental. For anxiety-heavy pieces, points readers in real distress toward proper support. No risky or one-size-fits-all directives.
- G3 Originality. Written from the founder's brain dump, not paraphrased from any source. Passes a similarity check. Contains at least one original frame, example, or structure.
- G4 Trust. Consistent named author (a business-owned pen name is acceptable), truthful bio, reachable about and contact pages.

**Scored dimensions (each 1 to 5; publish requires total >= 28 of 35, and pain-point fit, house voice, and structure each >= 4):**
1. Pain-point fit
2. Clarity and readability (about 8th-grade level)
3. House voice
4. Structure (calm-explainer shape)
5. Engagement (hook plus concrete anchor)
6. Actionability (one clear next step)
7. SEO hygiene and AI-citation readiness (intent match, title, 1 to 3 internal links, basic schema and entity signals, GEO levers per Section 7, no keyword stuffing)

---

## 6. Reviewer (second-AI) standing instruction

Run on every draft, in a separate context from the writer:

> You are a strict editor for a beginner-finance site. Score this draft against the rubric (4 hard gates, 7 scored dimensions). For each hard gate, return PASS or FAIL with the exact reason. Flag every factual or numeric claim that needs verifying. Flag any passage that risks reading like another source. Return a verdict of PUBLISH or REVISE, and if REVISE, list the specific fixes. Do not rewrite the article.

## 7. Engagement mechanics (evidence-based)

- Open an information gap (curiosity), but concrete, not vague clickbait. Vague curiosity-gap headlines underperform.
- Use one open loop (Zeigarnik effect) to pull the reader forward.
- Use one relatable individual scenario, not abstractions (narrative transportation; emotion drives attention and memory).
- Keep processing easy: plain words, short sentences and paragraphs.
- End with an email capture or next-article hook. Owned audience (email) is the real return-visit lever.
- AI-search resilience (GEO). Assume informational queries are increasingly answered in place by AI Overviews, so design to survive that. Three levers: (a) Be the cited source: include a credible statistic, cite primary sources, quote an authority only where exact wording sharpens meaning, write cleanly, and add schema/structured data plus consistent entity signals (the pen name, the about page). (b) Tilt topic intent toward angles AI cannot fully satisfy (decision pieces, comparisons, the reader's named fear and a lived scenario, original frames, tools) and away from bare definitional explainers, which are the most exposed. (c) Own the audience via email, the one channel AI does not sit between us and the reader. Refresh cornerstone pieces periodically, since recently updated content is cited more often. Being cited also lifts adjacent organic clicks, so GEO and SEO reinforce each other rather than competing.

Interview or brain dump (founder) -> draft in house voice (writer) -> independent review against rubric (reviewer) -> founder approves -> publish. The founder's raw input is what makes content original and human, which is what passes Google.
